# A Prescription for Readmission? What Your Meds Say About Your Risk

## Hook

As treatment plans grow more complex, patients may face unintended consequences. Could the very medications meant to help be increasing the liklehood of returning to the hospital?

## Problem statement 

Hospital readmissions within 30 days remain a persistent issue in healthcare, often reflecting difficulties in managing ongoing conditions after discharge. For patients, returning to the hospital can be financially burdensome, emotionally exhausting, and physically risky, as repeated hospital stays can lead to complications and disrupt recovery. This is especially true for individuals with diabetes, a chronic condition that requires constant monitoring and careful treatment. Managing diabetes often involves multiple medications and frequent dosage adjustments, creating complex treatment plans that can be difficult to maintain outside of a clinical setting. While these interventions are essential for controlling the disease, they may also signal underlying instability in a patient's health. This raises an important question: does greater treatment complexity, captured through the number and types of medications, as well as how often they are adjusted, contribute to a higher likelihood of readmission within 30 days?

## Solution Description 

This project focuses on identifying diabetic patients who may be at higher risk of readmission by examining patterns in their medication use, particularly the number of medications prescribed and how often those medications are adjusted. For patients with diabetes, treatment often involves carefully balancing multiple drugs to control blood sugar levels, and frequent changes can signal that a patient’s condition is not yet stable. As shown in the chart below, patients who experience more medication changes have noticeably higher readmission rates, suggesting that treatment instability is a key risk factor. By using this information, healthcare providers can better identify at-risk diabetic patients before discharge and take targeted actions, such as closer monitoring, clearer medication plans, or additional follow-up care, to help prevent avoidable readmissions.
